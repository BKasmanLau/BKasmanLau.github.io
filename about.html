Gym Programming

A guide on how to acheive your aesthetic goals

Posted by Brandon Lau on August 24, 2018

Creating a gym program is something only I recommend for intermediate to advanced lifters. If you are a beginner it is more beneficial to stick a tried and tested workout split to maximise strength and muscle mass. If you are a beginner skip down to the templates section to find the beginner workout split. If you are an intermediate to advanced lifter you can afford to customise your workout split to your needs. The things that I will discuss in this article well help you maximise your progress in the gym. There are three main princriples to follow: Adherence. Volume, Intensity and Frequency. Progression.

Adherence

It doesnt matter if you have the best workout program, if you don't go to the gym you wont make gains. This is why Adherence is the base of all training. All you have to remember is REF – realistic, enjoyable and flexible. It takes self-awareness and restraint to be able to appropriately implement these philosophies into program design, so be sure to “REF yourself” throughout the process. We are not robots, “optimal” is a concept that doesn’t always fit into our realities and the stresses we experience in life are largely out of our hands. Therefore, you need to be sure that the program you develop is specific to your individual life circumstances, considers your individual preferences, and is flexible enough to account for any curve balls life throws your way. As a student I like to workout in the mornings to get my day started, I haved adhered to this routine consistently over the past 2 years

Volume, Intensity, Frequency

Volume, intensity and frequency are what actually form the foundation of a successful program. These three variables are interrelated and inseparable from each other. Each affects the other, and can do so in different context. Volume: Volume is calculated by a simple formula. Weight * Reps Performed. If you are starting out as a beginner, you should be performing workouts with less volume. As you get more experienced you should increase your volume as your body adapts. Intensity: Intensity refers to how hard you are pushing yourself at the gym. For example if my one-max rep for bench press is 100kg. If I performed the bench press with a load of 95kg this would be considered high intensity. Conversely if performed the bench press with a load of 40kg this would be considered low intensity. Frequency: Frequency refers to the number of workouts you perform a week.

Progression

In order to keep getting bigger and stronger you need to gradually increase the training stimulus. This is called The Principle of Progressive Overload. If you are a novice or early stage intermediate lifter, simply setting up a training plan specific to your goals with an adequate work load will produce gains without much effort put into creating a plan to ensure progress. Simply selecting weights that challenge you on a session to session or week to week basis, even with the same set and repetition schemes within the week, will result in a gradual increase in strength and size. However, at a certain stage of your development, a plan for progression will become necessary for continued progress. I like to keep it simple. Increase my lifts by 2.5 pounds a week. If this isnt possible, try to squeeze out an extra rep each workout.

Too Long Didn't Read?

If you are a beginner follow my programe. Pick a program that isn't the most optimal but rather one you can stick to. If beginner keep volume low, intensity high, go to the gym 3 times a week.
